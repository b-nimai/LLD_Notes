# Introduction to Design Patterns

> **One-liner:** Design patterns are **time-tested, reusable blueprints** for recurring design problems — not copy-paste code, but proven *recipes* you adapt; the GoF cataloged **23**, grouped into **Creational / Structural / Behavioral**.

**Trigger phrase:** "Design pattern = a *recipe*, not the dish — don't reinvent the wheel for a recurring problem. 23 GoF patterns, 3 buckets: **Creational = how objects are *made*, Structural = how they're *composed*, Behavioral = how they *interact*.**"

> **Setup for this track:** patterns are **language-agnostic blueprints** (examples in Java). This folder covers **Creational** — Singleton & Factory first.

---

## Core Idea

A **design pattern** is a **standard, time-tested solution to a common software design problem**. It's an **abstract blueprint**, *not* a code template — it tells you the shape of the solution, you fill in the specifics.

**Why they matter:** they let you **avoid reinventing the wheel** for recurring design challenges, and they give a **shared vocabulary** ("just use a Factory here") that the whole team understands instantly.

### Real-life analogy
Patterns are like **cooking recipes**. To bake a cake you don't experiment from scratch each time — you follow a proven recipe. Patterns are tried-and-tested recipes for solving common coding problems consistently.

### Origin
Formalized by the **Gang of Four (GoF)** — Gamma, Helm, Johnson, Vlissides — in their 1994 book *"Design Patterns: Elements of Reusable Object-Oriented Software."* They cataloged **23 patterns** seen repeatedly in OO software, in **3 categories**.

---

## The Three Categories

| Category | About | Analogy | Pattern that captures it |
|----------|-------|---------|--------------------------|
| **Creational** | object **creation** — abstract *how* objects are made, so the system doesn't depend on it | vending machine: press "Orange Juice", don't care how it's prepared | **Factory** (creation logic hidden) |
| **Structural** | object **composition** — combine classes/objects into bigger structures, even incompatible ones | USB-C↔micro-USB **adapter** lets two devices work together | **Adapter** |
| **Behavioral** | object **interaction & responsibility** — how objects communicate with loose coupling | restaurant: waiter mediates between you and the chef | **Mediator** |

### The 23 patterns at a glance
- **Creational (5):** Singleton · Factory Method · Abstract Factory · Builder · Prototype
- **Structural (7):** Adapter · Bridge · Composite · Decorator · Facade · Flyweight · Proxy
- **Behavioral (11):** Observer · Strategy · Interpreter · Command · Chain of Responsibility · Mediator · State · Template Method · Visitor · Iterator · Memento

---

## Why it matters

- **Don't reinvent the wheel** — recurring problems already have vetted solutions.
- **Shared vocabulary** — "use a Singleton / Strategy here" communicates an entire design in two words.
- **Maintainable & scalable** — patterns bake in good OOP/SOLID, so systems absorb change cleanly.

---

## Pitfalls / Things to Remember

- **A pattern is a *blueprint*, not code.** Don't memorize an implementation — understand the *problem it solves* and adapt it.
- **Don't force a pattern.** Applying one where there's no recurring problem adds needless complexity (overengineering).
- **Know the 3 buckets cold** — Creational = *make*, Structural = *compose*, Behavioral = *interact*. The category tells you what problem the pattern targets.
- **Patterns lean on SOLID** — Factory/Strategy exist largely to honor OCP & DIP; weak SOLID = patterns won't click.
